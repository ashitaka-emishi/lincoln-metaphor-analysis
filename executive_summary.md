---
title: "Executive Summary"
---

This project is a structured analysis of Abraham Lincoln's metaphor system from 1838 to 1865. It asks how Lincoln's language made the Civil War appear not merely as a policy choice, but as an obligation imposed by oath, inheritance, national injury, democratic experiment, sacrifice, and Providence.

The central claim is that Lincoln's rhetoric repeatedly made mass violence appear obligatory without adopting the pathogen-group purification logic that Richard Koenigsberg identifies in genocidal political fantasy [@koenigsberg2009]. Lincoln's metaphors could authorize war, sacrifice, and national death, but they also retained possible exit conditions: wounds can heal, oaths can be fulfilled, experiments can be decided, debts can be paid, and divine punishment can run its course.

The project combines two interpretive layers. Conceptual Metaphor Theory identifies the linguistic mappings that organize Lincoln's political rhetoric [@lakoff1980]. Koenigsberg's ideological-fantasy analysis then asks what political-psychological work those metaphors may perform: what they frame as obligatory, what forms of violence they authorize, what sacrifices they redeem, and what human agents they erase [@koenigsberg2009].

The corpus contains 28 documents across Lincoln's public career, including speeches, letters, debates, fragments, congressional messages, and legal documents. Each text is segmented into stable sentence-level JSON, annotated for metaphor instances, and prepared for corpus-wide analysis.

The project's significance for political pathology is methodological as well as interpretive. It shows how the symbolic structures that authorize political violence can be tracked systematically across a corpus, while preserving the close-reading sensitivity needed for historical and psychoanalytic interpretation.

## Authorship and Method Note

This project is the work of Andrew Hammer, a human engineer/researcher designing and directing an agentic AI-assisted scholarly analysis system. AI tools are used as instruments for corpus preparation, structured annotation, validation, synthesis support, and website generation. Interpretive responsibility, research design, and scholarly framing remain human-authored.

The corpus annotation (136 instances across 28 documents), corpus-wide analysis, controlled outputs, reliability workflow, and claim-audit layers are implemented. The publication-facing analysis and synthesis documents have passed the documented publication review; reviewers should begin with the [Publication Package](publication_package.md) for reproducibility commands, data availability, limitations, and AI-use statement.
