title: "Executive Summary"
---

This project is a structured analysis of Abraham Lincoln's metaphor system from 1838 to 1865. It asks how Lincoln's language made the Civil War appear not merely as a policy choice, but as an obligation imposed by oath, inheritance, national injury, democratic experiment, sacrifice, and Providence.

The central claim is that Lincoln's rhetoric made mass violence morally necessary without adopting the purification logic that Richard Koenigsberg identifies in genocidal political fantasy. Lincoln's metaphors could authorize war, sacrifice, and national death, but they also retained exit conditions: wounds can heal, oaths can be fulfilled, experiments can be decided, debts can be paid, and divine punishment can run its course.

The project combines two methods. Conceptual Metaphor Theory identifies the linguistic mappings that organize Lincoln's political imagination: nation as body, Union as covenant, republic as experiment, war as birth, founders as inheritance, and history as Providence. Koenigsberg's ideological fantasy analysis then asks what psychological and political work those metaphors perform: what they make obligatory, what forms of violence they authorize, what sacrifices they redeem, and what human agents they erase.

The corpus contains 28 documents across Lincoln's public career, including speeches, letters, debates, fragments, congressional messages, and legal documents. Each text is segmented into stable sentence-level JSON, annotated for metaphor instances, and prepared for corpus-wide analysis.

The project's significance for political pathology is methodological as well as interpretive. It shows how the symbolic structures that authorize political violence can be tracked systematically across a corpus, while preserving the close-reading sensitivity needed for historical and psychoanalytic interpretation.
