## Primary Finding: The Corpus's Most Active Founding-Generation Language

inst_00126 introduces the most active founding-generation language in the corpus: "when the fathers of the government **cut off the source of slavery** by the abolition of the slave trade."

In all other cluster_05 instances, the founding fathers either:
- **placed** slavery somewhere (Ottawa, Jonesboro, Alton)
- **left** it on a basis (Jonesboro, Quincy p05_s03)
- **expected and intended** it to end (Alton)
- **eschewed and rejected** naming it (Peoria)

At Quincy, the fathers are described as actively **cutting off the source** — severing the supply line of slavery by abolishing the international slave trade. This is the founding-generation as agents of pruning/severing, not merely placers or leavers. The "cut off the source" language introduces a severing sub-metaphor within cluster_05 that has no parallel elsewhere in the corpus.

## ext_022: The P04 Arc — Contested Attribution + Active Founding Agency

The two sentences of ext_022 work together:

1. **inst_00125** (p04_s06, anchor): Lincoln contests Douglas's attribution — the fathers did NOT choose to make the nation part slave and part free as a matter of preference; they left it that way because they knew no way to get rid of it.

2. **inst_00126** (p04_s07, conclusion): Lincoln demonstrates positive founding agency — the fathers actively CUT OFF the source of slavery (abolition of slave trade) and adopted a system of restriction. The contrast with Douglas's claim is sharp: the fathers were not passive endorsers of the status quo but active restricters of slavery's growth.

Together: the fathers did NOT choose the divided state (p04_s06) + the fathers actively restricted slavery's source (p04_s07). This combination is Lincoln's fullest account of founding agency on slavery at Quincy.

## inst_00127: The Cotton Gin Basis Contrast

"Judge Douglas could not let it stand upon **the basis upon which our fathers placed it**, but removed it and put it upon the cotton gin basis."

This is the corpus's clearest two-basis contrast: the founding basis (moral/political restriction toward extinction) vs. the cotton gin basis (technological-economic necessity for perpetuation). Douglas replaced the founding basis with a technological one — making slavery's perpetuation a function of economic necessity rather than founding inheritance. Lincoln's argument: the cotton gin basis is not a legitimate basis because it substitutes economic convenience for founding principle.

## inst_00128: Henry Clay as Near-Founding Authority

Threshold annotation (conf 0.72). Lincoln invokes Henry Clay (1777-1852) as the transmitted authority for the Declaration's equality principle. When Douglas charges Lincoln with adopting a "heretical statement" in the Declaration of Independence, Lincoln responds: "I have only uttered the sentiments that Henry Clay used to hold."

Clay bridges the founding generation (he was a colleague of Madison, Jefferson, and Adams) and the antebellum era (he died in 1852, six years before this debate). Lincoln learned his politics in the Whig tradition Clay led. Invoking Clay converts Lincoln's position from personal innovation to inherited Whig orthodoxy — and simultaneously charges Douglas with retroactively condemning his own party's tradition.
