## Primary Finding: Lincoln Theorizing His Own Metaphor System

This fragment is the most meta-analytically significant document in the corpus: Lincoln is explicitly theorizing the relationship between his clusters, not just deploying them.

**The mapping** (stated explicitly by Lincoln):
- Apple of gold = the Declaration's principle of "Liberty to all" → **cluster_03**
- Picture of silver = the Union and Constitution → **cluster_02**
- The picture frames and preserves the apple → the covenant exists to serve the proposition
- "The picture was made for the apple — not the apple for the picture"

**What this tells us about the cluster hierarchy:**

cluster_03 (experiment/proposition) is foundational. The Declaration's assertion — "all men are created equal" — is the apple. The Union and Constitution (cluster_02) are the institutional frame built to preserve it. Cluster_05 (founding fathers) created the apple through sacrifice. Cluster_01 (body/organism) appears when the apple-picture is threatened with damage ("bruised").

The hierarchy: **Liberty proposition (cluster_03) → covenant institutions (cluster_02) → damage/preservation (cluster_01)**. cluster_04 (birth/creation) and cluster_06 (Providence) are both absent from this fragment — this is pre-war Lincoln, before the birth and theodicy framings are activated.

## The Stephens Connection

The Proverbs 25:11 metaphor was used by Alexander Stephens in his letter to Lincoln dated December 30, 1860 — before Lincoln wrote this fragment. This creates a remarkable intertextual situation: Lincoln may be appropriating his future Confederate Vice-President's metaphor to articulate the anti-slavery logic of the Declaration. Lincoln takes Stephens's image and inverts its implications: for Stephens, constitutional protection of slavery is the frame; for Lincoln, Liberty is the apple that the Constitution must serve.

## The Priority Inversion as Rhetorical Weapon

The aphorism "the picture was made for the apple — not the apple for the picture" (inst_00041) is structurally identical to two other Lincoln inversions annotated in the corpus:
- Hodges Letter: "a life is never wisely given to save a limb" (inst_00031)
- Greeley Letter: "save the Union...not either to save or to destroy slavery"

All three use the same logical structure: establish two things in a relationship, then state the correct priority order, then forbid the inversion. This is Lincoln's characteristic argumentative move — not just making an argument but making the opposite argument seem absurd.

## Cluster_01 Entering the Frame (inst_00042)

The final instance ("blurred, or bruised or broken") is analytically important because it shows cluster_01 (body/organism) being imported into what has been an object/aesthetic metaphor. "Bruised" is bodily damage — it breaks the silver-frame aesthetic and adds organic vulnerability to the apple-picture frame.

This is how Lincoln's clusters interact: the body metaphor is available to colonize any other metaphor when damage/threat is invoked. Even a precious-object metaphor becomes organic when threatened.

## Diachronic Position

Document is Phase 1 (pre-war, c. January 1861) — written immediately before the First Inaugural, in the interregnum period between Lincoln's election and inauguration.

**Clusters activated**: cluster_01 (minor, via "bruised"), cluster_02, cluster_03, cluster_05  
**Clusters absent**: cluster_04 (birth/creation), cluster_06 (Providence/theodicy)

The absence of cluster_04 and cluster_06 is diagnostic: in January 1861, Lincoln has not yet activated the birth or theodicy framings. The war generates both. The fragment's metaphor system is pre-war: preservation-focused (cluster_02, cluster_03) rather than creation-focused (cluster_04) or punishment-focused (cluster_06).
