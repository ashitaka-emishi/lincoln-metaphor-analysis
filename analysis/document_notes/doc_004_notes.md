## Primary Finding: Highest Cluster_05 Density in Pre-War Corpus

The Peoria Speech (October 1854) has the highest cluster_05 instance count of any pre-war document (5 instances), exceeding Cooper Union's 4. The founding fathers are deployed across the full range of the cluster_05 frame:

1. **Juridical catalogue** (inst_00106, p87_s01): the founding generation's complete legislative portfolio as binding precedent
2. **Avoidance-as-precedent** (inst_00107, p116_s04): the founders' deliberate omission of slavery language as inherited proprietary standard
3. **Inherited constraint** (inst_00109, p116_s12): the founding compromise as the minimum floor and maximum ceiling of inherited obligation
4. **Departure-from-beginning** (inst_00110, p124_s03): the eighty-year trajectory measured against the founding declaration
5. **Re-adoption imperative** (inst_00112, p127_s05): the inheritance as a recoverable standard that must be restored

Together these five instances span cluster_05 from historical-juridical to restorative mode. The Peoria Speech is the document where Lincoln most fully develops the founding-fathers-as-binding-precedent argument.

## The Anaphoric Founding Catalogue (inst_00106)

The Peoria Speech's rhetorically most powerful cluster_05 instance is the anaphoric catalogue at p87_s01: "This same generation of men...who declared this principle---who declared independence---who fought the war of the revolution through---who afterwards made the constitution under which we still live---these same men passed the ordinance of '87." The six-part anaphora ("who declared...who declared...who fought...who afterwards made...these same men") enumerates the founding generation's complete portfolio to demonstrate that the same people who established the republic also restricted slavery. This is Lincoln's direct rebuttal to Douglas's claim that the founders endorsed popular sovereignty on slavery.

## The P116 Arc: Three Cluster Instances in One Paragraph

Paragraph p116 contains three closely related instances (p116_s04, p116_s11, p116_s12) that form the most concentrated analytical sequence in the Peoria Speech:

- **p116_s04** (inst_00107, cluster_05): The founders AVOIDED naming slavery — they eschewed and rejected it.
- **p116_s11** (inst_00108, cluster_01): They HID it — like an afflicted man hiding a cancer to avoid immediate surgery.
- **p116_s12** (inst_00109, cluster_05): This was the minimum they COULD do and the maximum they WOULD do.

The three sentences together constitute Lincoln's fullest account of the founding compromise on slavery: the founders' strategy was deliberate avoidance (cluster_05) → medical deferral (cluster_01) → inherited floor-and-ceiling constraint (cluster_05). The Kansas-Nebraska Act violates all three: it names slavery, removes the deferral by legitimizing it, and exceeds the founders' maximum.

## inst_00108: Most Medically Explicit Cluster_01 Instance in Pre-War Corpus

"The thing is hid away, in the constitution, just as an afflicted man hides away a wen or a cancer, which he dares not cut out at once, lest he bleed to death; with the promise, nevertheless, that the cutting may begin at the end of a given time."

This is the most medically explicit cluster_01 instance in the pre-war corpus. Key features:
- **The political body**: the constitution = the afflicted man's body
- **The disease**: slavery = cancer/wen within the body
- **The cure**: surgery (cutting out) = eventual elimination of slavery
- **The deferral logic**: immediate surgery would cause fatal bleeding → the body cannot survive immediate cure
- **The promise**: the deferred surgery will eventually be performed

This is NOT disease_and_purification in Koenigsberg's sense. The disease is an INSTITUTION (slavery), not a human group. The cure is surgical removal of the institution, not elimination of a contaminated people. The afflicted man is sympathetically depicted — he is forced to defer surgery, not endorsing the cancer.

The Kansas-Nebraska Act, in Lincoln's analysis, breaks the deferred-surgery promise by legitimizing the cancer rather than scheduling its removal.

## inst_00111: Proto-Sacrificial Blood (p127_s03)

"Let us turn and wash it white, in the spirit, if not the blood, of the Revolution."

This is the first suggestion in the corpus that national purification may require blood. The structure: "in the spirit, if not the blood, of the Revolution" — the primary washing agent is the revolutionary spirit (returning to founding principles), but the blood of the Revolution is named as a possible alternative. This introduces the logic that will develop into Gettysburg's "last full measure of devotion" and the Second Inaugural's "terrible war": the stain of slavery on the national body may require blood to wash clean.

The "wash it white" is NOT disease_and_purification: the stain is the national sin of slavery expansion, not a human group. The national body's guilt (for departing from the founding) is what requires cleansing. The washing agent (revolutionary spirit/blood) is the founding inheritance, not an elimination of a contaminated people.

## The Self-Government Argument (p77–p100): Why Not Cluster_03

The Peoria Speech contains Lincoln's most extended argument about self-government (p77–p100, roughly 25 paragraphs), culminating at p79_s02–s04: "When the white man governs himself, AND also governs another man, that is more than self-government---that is despotism." This is a powerful logical argument about democratic principles. However, it is not annotated as cluster_03 (republic as experiment/proposition) because:

- The argument proves a DEFINITION (what self-government means) rather than testing a PROPOSITION (whether self-government can survive)
- The logic is ethical-definitional, not experimental-empirical
- Lincoln argues from the meaning of terms, not from the republic as a hypothesis being tested

cluster_03 requires the republic-as-experiment frame: the war/crisis as a test of whether democratic self-government can succeed. In 1854, Lincoln has not yet reached that frame — he argues from what self-government IS, not from whether it CAN survive.

## Absent Clusters

- **cluster_02 (covenant/oath) absent**: Despite Lincoln's reference to "national faith" at p109_s02, the covenant-as-sacred-bond frame is below threshold. Lincoln argues through founding precedent and logical consistency, not through plighted faith.
- **cluster_03 (experiment/proposition) absent**: The self-government argument is definitional, not experimental. No "testing whether" formula.
- **cluster_04 (birth/creation) absent**: No nativity/birth frame. The republic has been founded; the crisis is about whether it will honor its founding inheritance.
- **cluster_06 (Providence/theodicy) absent**: Despite invoking moral absolutes, Lincoln argues through human logic and founding authority. No divine sanction frame.
