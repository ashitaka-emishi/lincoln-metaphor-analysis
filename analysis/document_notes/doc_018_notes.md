## Primary Finding: The Cognitive Minimum

The Blind Memorandum is Lincoln's most stripped text — two sentences written privately, sealed, unseen by anyone including Lincoln himself in any meaningful sense until after the election. No rhetorical audience. No political staging. This is as close as the corpus gets to Lincoln's unmediated cognition.

In that context, the one metaphorical formulation that survives is "save the Union." Not "bind up the nation's wounds." Not "this nation, under God." Not "the proposition." Just "save the Union" — the same formulation as the Greeley Letter, which was itself a suppression document.

**The inference**: "Save the Union" is not primarily a rhetorical device. It is Lincoln's actual cognitive frame for the war. The elaborated metaphors (body, birth, covenant, proposition, Providence) are the public architecture built on top of a private foundation that is simply this: the Union can be lost, and it must be saved.

## Diachronic and Register Significance

Written August 23, 1864 — eleven days before the Greeley Letter's anniversary, twenty months after Gettysburg, six and a half months before the Second Inaugural. The date puts it at the nadir of Lincoln's political fortunes (Sherman had not yet taken Atlanta; the Democratic peace platform was being drafted).

At this moment, when Lincoln expects to lose the election and contemplates handing over a losing war to a successor committed to peace, his private formulation is still "save the Union." Not: "the experiment must not fail." Not: "the new birth must be delivered." Just: save it.

Register: fragment_private — the most restricted register in the corpus. Confidence 0.60 documents the borderline: "save" may be entirely dead language here. The annotation is retained because the cross-register persistence of "save the Union" (public address → semi-public letter → private fragment) is itself the finding.

## Koenigsberg Constructs

**Fantasy type**: oath_and_obligation — Lincoln frames cooperation with a hypothetical McClellan successor as "duty," even when he expects to have lost and expects McClellan to be unable to complete the task.

**Violence logic**: obligatory — the war must continue because the covenant obligation persists through electoral defeat.

**Obligatory frame**: strongest in the corpus at the private level. Lincoln is not here addressing an audience he needs to persuade. He is recording his own obligation to himself. This confirms that the obligatory frame is not audience-constructed but internalized.

**lincoln_non_agent**: flagged. Even in this private document, Lincoln positions himself as bound by duty rather than choosing. He does not say "I will try to save the Union." He says "it will be my duty to so co-operate." Duty is ascribed to him from outside; he is its instrument, not its author.

**Sacrificial economy**: absent — no soldier deaths, no productive sacrifice logic. The stripped nature of the document means even the transactional structure is missing.

## Annotator Notes

- The editorial apparatus (section 2, annotated with `authorship_note: "editorial_apparatus"`) contains richer language: "try to save the country" (Hay's paraphrase of Lincoln's reported speech), "final trial," "all is lost." These are not annotatable under protocol since they are Hay's transcription, not Lincoln's text. However, the contrast is notable: Hay's paraphrase of Lincoln's cabinet speech is more metaphorically rich than Lincoln's sealed private memo — suggesting Lincoln reserved the elaborated language for social/performative contexts.
- The sealing ritual itself is an oath performance: Lincoln bound his cabinet members as witnesses to a commitment they could not read. This parallels the covenant cluster logic without being linguistic metaphor — an enacted covenant rather than a spoken one.
- Confidence 0.60 is the lowest in the corpus so far. If "save" is dead language here, doc_018 is a zero-instance document. The borderline is documented so future analysts can reconsider the instance if the corpus-wide confidence calibration shifts.
