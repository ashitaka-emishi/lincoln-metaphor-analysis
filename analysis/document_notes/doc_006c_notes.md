## Primary Finding: Cluster_05-Organized Debate; Founding-Position Argument as Spine

The Jonesboro debate (September 15, 1858) is organized around Douglas's question "Why can't we let it stand as our fathers placed it?" — and Lincoln's entire speech is his answer: because Douglas and his friends have changed it from where the fathers placed it. The founding-fathers argument is the rhetorical spine of Lincoln's Jonesboro speech.

The ext_021 arc (p04) is the fullest statement of the founding-as-original-position argument across the debates: three adjacent sentences trace the argument from departure (p04_s04) through policy (p04_s06) to the restorative imperative (p04_s08). Together they constitute the Jonesboro debate's central analytical claim.

## ext_020: The House Divided Props Arc (p15)

Lincoln's most architecturally explicit use of the House Divided metaphor in the debate corpus appears at p15:

"[Regional differences] don't make the house **a house divided against itself**. They are **the props that hold up the house and sustain the Union**."

The argument structure:
- **Douglas's premise**: Regional variety (different laws for sugar, flour, etc.) creates division
- **Lincoln's counter**: Regional variety does NOT divide the house — these differences are the PROPS that sustain the Union
- **The implicit conclusion**: Only slavery makes the house divided against itself

This is the clearest architectural elaboration of the House Divided metaphor in the debate corpus. The original June 1858 speech uses the metaphor as a structural warning; here Lincoln uses it analytically: the house has props (regional diversity) and a dividing force (slavery). The props sustain it; the dividing force threatens it.

## ext_021: The P04 Founding-Position Arc — The Jonesboro Spine

Paragraph p04 contains Lincoln's fullest debate-floor answer to "why can't we let it stand as our fathers placed it?":

1. **inst_00120** (p04_s04, anchor): "position in which our fathers originally placed it" — Lincoln's answer: Douglas changed it from that position. The 'position' language makes the founding settlement spatial and measurable: there is a location where the fathers placed slavery, and Douglas moved it.

2. **inst_00121** (p04_s06, policy): "policy of its founders to prohibit the spread of slavery into the new Territories" — The founders' position was not passive tolerance but an active policy of prohibition. This converts the inheritance from a vague precedent into a specific legislative mandate.

3. **inst_00122** (p04_s08, conclusion): "basis that the fathers of our government originally placed it upon" — The restorative imperative: place it BACK AGAIN on the founding basis. The architectural 'basis' language (compare Ottawa inst_00114) and the explicit 'back again' signal that the foundation exists and must be reclaimed, not created anew.

Together: the fathers placed slavery in a position (spatial) → that position was an active policy of prohibition (procedural) → the goal is to place it back again on that basis (restorative). This three-step argument is the Jonesboro debate's analytical core.

## inst_00123: Douglas Changed the Founding Basis (p05_s06)

"He insisted, therefore, upon its being changed from **the basis upon which the Fathers of the Government left it** to the basis of its perpetuation and nationalization."

This individual instance captures the departure-from-founding-basis argument in its sharpest form: the contrast between "the basis upon which the Fathers left it" (restriction on course of extinction) and "the basis of its perpetuation and nationalization" (Douglas's position) is fully explicit. The capitalization of "Fathers of the Government" (reflecting the original text) emphasizes the institutional weight of the founding authority.

## Why cluster_03 and cluster_06 Are Absent

The Jonesboro debate is the most southerly debate site (southern Illinois) — Lincoln's audience is his least sympathetic. His rhetorical strategy is to stay on founding-fathers ground (cluster_05) and the house-divided structural argument (cluster_01), which have bipartisan appeal. He does not invoke the experiment/proposition frame (cluster_03), which appeals to democratic universalism, or the Providence frame (cluster_06), which has a more evangelical register. Both would have been politically risky at Jonesboro.
