## Metadata Discrepancy

doc_014 metadata date is 1862-12-01, but the body text explicitly reads "December 3, 1861" and the closing signature reads "December 3, 1861 ABRAHAM LINCOLN." Content confirms December 1861: (1) p71 discusses Scott's recent retirement (Scott retired November 1, 1861); (2) the Maryland/Kentucky/Missouri analysis describes year-one-of-war developments; (3) there is no discussion of the Emancipation Proclamation or compensated emancipation amendment proposals, which would dominate a December 1862 message. The actual December 1, 1862 Annual Message (Phase 3) — which contained "we cannot escape history" and the compensated emancipation constitutional amendment proposal — is likely another document in the corpus. Annotation uses December 3, 1861 (Phase 2).

## Primary Finding: Minimal Metaphor Density in Administrative Register

At 3 instances across 224 sentences, doc_014 has the lowest metaphor density in the corpus for a high-analytical-priority document. This is a register finding: congressional administrative messages are not rhetorical addresses. The document is enumeration — appropriations, navy, judiciary reorganization, territories, labor/capital theory. Lincoln's metaphor system operates below the surface of the administrative content. The two cluster_03 instances appear in explicitly evaluative passages (p76 and p84) where Lincoln steps outside administrative enumeration to frame the war's meaning.

Active clusters: 03, 06 (two). Absent: 01, 02, 04, 05 — all four of the clusters that require rhetorical address mode.

## inst_00095 (p76_s01): Insurrection as Attack on a Proposition

"It continues to develop that the insurrection is largely, if not exclusively, a war upon the first principle of popular government---the rights of the people." This converts Confederate secession from a political dispute about constitutional interpretation into a logical attack: the insurrection is not disagreeing about policy but assaulting the foundational proposition of democratic self-government. The phrase "continues to develop" treats the framing as an emerging empirical finding, not a rhetorical choice — the evidence is accumulating that this is the war's true nature.

This is the second cluster_03 instance in which Lincoln frames the war as a proposition-being-tested (compare inst_00087, doc_010, July 4 Message): the first framed the war as a question to be answered; this frames the insurrection as the challenge to a first principle whose truth must be defended.

## inst_00096 (p84_s03): The 70-Year Census Proof

The most data-grounded cluster_03 activation in the corpus. Lincoln presents the 70-year population growth record (8x increase from 1790 to 1860 census) and then characterizes it as what "the popular principle applied to government...has produced in a given time." The census data becomes the experimental output of the democratic proposition; the measurement language ("in a given time," "what...it promises for the future") converts demographic statistics into scientific results.

This experimental frame converts the war's stakes: destroying the Union would not merely end a political arrangement but would erase the proven experimental record. The 70-year cumulative results of the democratic experiment would be forfeit.

## The Labor/Capital Passage (p78–p83): Unannotated but Analytically Significant

The most intellectually distinctive passage in doc_014 is the labor/capital argument (p78–p83). Lincoln directly attacks the Southern assumption that labor is only available in connection with capital (p78_s03), argues that "Labor is prior to, and independent of, capital" (p81_s01), and describes the free-labor system as one that "opens the way to all---gives hope to all" (p82_s04).

This passage is not annotated as cluster metaphor (no cluster maps to the labor/capital domain), but it is a key finding for the Koenigsberg analysis: Lincoln is constructing the ideological architecture that makes the war feel necessary as a defense of free institutions. The free-labor system is the human positive that the war defends; enslaved people appear only as the negative contrast (bought vs. hired). The passage is the clearest pre-Emancipation statement of what Lincoln is fighting to preserve — and what he does not name (the enslaved people's own freedom as the positive value).
