## Primary Finding: The Corpus's Clearest Dual-Cluster Instance

inst_00124 is the clearest dual-cluster instance in the corpus so far: cluster_05 (Jefferson as founding authority) is the primary frame, with cluster_06 (divine justice/theodicy) co-activated by the content of Jefferson's cited words.

The sentence: "[While Mr. Jefferson was the owner of slaves,] in speaking upon this very subject, he used the strong language that **'he trembled for his country when he remembered that God was just'**; and I will offer the highest premium in my power to Judge Douglas if he will show that he, in all his life, ever uttered a sentiment at all akin to that of Jefferson."

The dual-cluster logic:
- **cluster_05 (primary)**: Jefferson is the founding authority whose words constitute the inherited standard; Lincoln cites Jefferson to demonstrate that Douglas falls below that standard
- **cluster_06 (co-activated)**: The content of Jefferson's words invokes divine justice — God's judgment on slavery is what Jefferson acknowledged and Douglas never has

This instance is also notable because the founding authority Lincoln invokes acknowledged the moral gravity of slavery before God — while himself owning enslaved people. Lincoln uses this contradiction to maximum effect: even the founders who owned enslaved people trembled before God's justice. Douglas, who owns no enslaved people and claims neutrality on slavery, has never approached this moral seriousness.

## The Challenge Formula

"I will offer the highest premium in my power to Judge Douglas if he will show that he, in all his life, ever uttered a sentiment at all akin to that of Jefferson."

This challenge formula is rhetorically powerful: it frames the inheritance dispute as a measurable test. Lincoln is not claiming to be as great as Jefferson — he is claiming that Douglas falls below the founding generation's own moral standard on slavery. The inheritance is used not to elevate Lincoln but to expose Douglas's moral insufficiency.

## Why Only One Instance in 268 Sentences

The Galesburg debate focuses heavily on:
1. The Declaration of Independence dispute (whether "all men are created equal" includes Black people) — factual/definitional, not metaphor-rich
2. The Dred Scott/Supreme Court authority dispute (whether Lincoln is bound by Dred Scott) — legal argumentation
3. The "alliance with Trumbull" charge — factual rebuttal

These are argumentative registers that minimize metaphor density. The founding-fathers argument appears only where Lincoln invokes Jefferson to show Douglas's moral inadequacy.
