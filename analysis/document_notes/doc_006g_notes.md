## Primary Finding: The Richest Debate — House Divided Fully Articulated and Cancer Metaphor Extended

Alton (October 15, 1858) is the richest debate for metaphor density (8 instances) and the analytically most significant. Two major discoveries:

### 1. ext_023: The Fullest House Divided Statement in the Corpus

The p33 arc (inst_00129/130/131) is the only place in the corpus where Lincoln quotes the House Divided metaphor directly AND extends it through three steps:

- **p33_s04** (anchor): "``A house divided against itself cannot stand.'' I believe this government cannot endure permanently half Slave and half Free." — The explicit Springfield quotation followed immediately by its restatement in biological terms ("endure permanently"). The architectural (house/stand) and biological (endure) variants are fused.

- **p33_s05** (reassurance): "I do not expect the house to fall---but I do expect it will cease to be divided." — This directly rebuts the charge that Lincoln's House Divided thesis implies disunion. The house will not fall; it will cease to be divided. Lincoln distinguishes between collapse and resolution.

- **p33_s06** (resolution): "It will become all one thing, or all the other." — The structural resolution formula: uniformity is the only stable condition for the house. The ambiguity of which resolution is intentional.

### 2. inst_00132: The Novel Cancer-Spreading Variant (p56_s07)

"You may have a wen or a cancer upon your person and not be able to cut it out lest you bleed to death; but surely it is no way to cure it, to engraft it and spread it over your whole body."

This is the second major cancer/wen instance in the corpus (after Peoria's inst_00108), and analytically distinct:

| | Peoria inst_00108 (1854) | Alton inst_00132 (1858) |
|--|--|--|
| Cancer = | Slavery hidden in the constitutional body | Slavery as disease on the body politic |
| Immediate surgery? | Not possible — fatal bleeding | Not possible — fatal bleeding |
| The founders' strategy | Deferral (hide it, promise eventual surgery) | — |
| The wrong response | Kansas-Nebraska breaks the surgical promise | Spreading the cancer over the whole body |
| The argument | Don't break the deferral promise | Don't expand the disease as a cure |

The Alton variant is more rhetorically aggressive: it directly rebuts the pro-slavery expansion argument ("spreading slavery is necessary for its health and eventual peaceful ending") with the cancer metaphor: you don't cure a cancer by spreading it — you cure it by containing it (non-extension) and eventually removing it.

## ext_024: Founding Placement = Public Mind Settlement (p38 Arc)

The ext_024 arc introduces the fullest account of what the founding placement accomplished:

- **p38_s04**: Placing slavery where the fathers originally placed it → the public mind would believe in ultimate extinction
- **p38_s09**: The fathers placed that institution where the public mind DID rest in the belief of ultimate extinction

The inheritance is not just a legal/policy position but a psychological-political one. The founding placement created the public mind's BELIEF in ultimate extinction — a belief that the Kansas-Nebraska Act disturbed by disturbing the placement. The full inheritance includes both the policy condition and the psychological peace it produced.

## inst_00135: The Founding Intention (p43_s05)

"the fathers of the Government **expected and intended** the institution of slavery to come to an end."

This is the corpus's strongest teleological cluster_05 claim: not just that the founders placed slavery on the course of extinction (factual/positional) but that they EXPECTED AND INTENDED it to end. Lincoln's evidence: the founders used covert language in the Constitution (avoiding naming slavery) specifically so that when slavery was gone, the document would show no trace of it. The intention creates a stronger inherited obligation: you must honor not just what the founders did, but what they expected and intended to follow from it.
