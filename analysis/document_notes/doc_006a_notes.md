## Primary Finding: Cluster_05-Only Document; Debate Context Shapes Frame

Ottawa (August 21, 1858) is the only document in the annotated corpus so far with a single cluster present (cluster_05). All four instances invoke the founding fathers as the authoritative standard for slavery policy. The debate format explains this: Lincoln is responding to Douglas's claim to "original principles," and his entire rhetorical strategy is to contest who has the legitimate founding inheritance.

Notably absent in the Ottawa debate compared to other Phase 1 documents:
- **cluster_01 absent**: No body/organism metaphors. Lincoln argues through precedent and jurisprudence, not through the nation-as-body frame.
- **cluster_03 absent**: No experiment/proposition frame. Ottawa precedes the Cooper Union (1860) reactivation of cluster_03.
- **cluster_06 absent**: No Providence frame. Lincoln's authority is the founders, not God.
- **cluster_02 absent**: No covenant/oath frame. Despite the debate stakes, Lincoln argues from founding precedent not sacred obligation.

## inst_00113: The Founding Triad as Bipartisan Authority (p23_s07)

"Now, I believe if we could arrest the spread, and place it where **Washington, and Jefferson, and Madison placed it**, it would be in the course of ultimate extinction"

The triad deploys bipartisan founding authority: Washington (Federalist), Jefferson (Democratic-Republican), Madison (framer of the Constitution). All three are cited together to show that the founding generation as a whole — across factional lines — placed slavery on the course of ultimate extinction. This is Lincoln's direct counter to Douglas's claim that popular sovereignty follows founding principles: no single faction can claim the founders; they all agreed on restriction.

## ext_019: The P27 Arc — Lincoln's Most Complete Debate-Floor Founding Argument

Paragraph p27 contains three cluster_05 instances (inst_00114/115/116) that constitute Lincoln's fullest debate-floor articulation of the founding-as-original-basis argument:

1. **inst_00114** (p27_s07, anchor): "the original basis---the basis upon which our fathers placed it" — The founding position as architectural foundation; departing from it creates instability.
2. **inst_00115** (p27_s09, challenge): "the plan of the founders of government" — A rhetorical question: does Douglas actually claim to follow the founders' plan? Exposes the gap between Douglas's claim and the founding record.
3. **inst_00116** (p27_s11, conclusion): "fighting it in the Jeffersonian, Washingtonian, and Madisonian fashion" — Lincoln's own strategy is positioned as continuous with the founders' method, not innovative beyond it.

The three sentences form a complete argument: (1) the founding is the original basis; (2) Douglas cannot claim founding authority; (3) Lincoln's resistance follows the founders' own fashion. This is the Ottawa debate's argumentative spine.

## inst_00116: Adjectival Conversion of Founder Names

"I am fighting it upon these ``original principles''---fighting it in **the Jeffersonian, Washingtonian, and Madisonian fashion**."

The adjectival form ("Jeffersonian, Washingtonian, and Madisonian") converts the founding fathers' names into methodological descriptors — a rhetorically compressed inheritance claim. Compare inst_00113, where the same founders appear as a noun catalogue ("Washington, and Jefferson, and Madison placed it"). The adjectival form makes the founders into an inherited style: Lincoln is not just citing them but embodying their method.

## Why So Few Instances?

The Ottawa debate is 5,776 lines of text with 316 Lincoln-authored sentences — a long document with relatively few annotated instances (4). The explanation:

- Most of Lincoln's Ottawa speech is direct rebuttal of Douglas's specific charges (his five questions, his accusations about the Bloomington platform, etc.). This is argumentative prose, not metaphor-heavy rhetorical address.
- The founding-father cluster_05 instances appear at the moments when Lincoln steps back from point-by-point rebuttal to make his positive case: these are the argumentative peaks.
- The debate format constrains the register: Lincoln cannot sustain extended metaphor in the way he can in a prepared address (Peoria, Cooper Union). The density of cluster_05 is lower here than in the Peoria Speech.
- No candidates above threshold for cluster_01, 02, 03, 04, or 06 were found in the 316 Lincoln-authored sentences.
